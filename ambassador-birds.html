Ambassador Birds

Our Ambassadors

Our non-releasable Ambassador Birds are living out their natural lives comfortably — with room service — in safe and private enclosures at MARS Wildlife Rescue.

Some of the birds who have become Ambassadors suffered devastating injuries that prevented them from being returned to the wild, while others have become habituated to humans for various reasons. All have found new purpose helping to educate the public about wild nature. To keep these birds, MARS applied for permits and continues to meet government regulations for their appropriate care.

Marlowe

BARRED OWL (STRIX VARIA)

Marlowe came to MARS in the fall of 2019 after hitting a window in Comox in the middle of the night. The home owners found him and brought him to our wildlife hospital the next day. He showed many signs of severe head trauma, and we weren’t sure if he could even see properly. He showed no threat avoidance, and was extremely docile.

He saw several vets who determined there was no reason Marlowe should not be able to see, and that it was most likely the head trauma that was causing him not to react to movement or oncoming threats. He struggled to eat for the first several months with us, and progressed from being force-fed, to handfed, to eventually eating on his own without any help from the caregivers.

Marlowe has progressed a great deal since arriving with us as a patient, but nowhere near enough to be successfully released into the wild. He gets his name from Christopher Marlowe who was William Shakespeare’s friend, collaborator, and (at times) rival.

Marlowe is a very gentle bird who was born in the spring of 2019, with an expected lifespan of upwards of 20 years in captivity. He typically weighs about 600 – 700 grams, and eats two mice a day.

Barred Owl Cool Facts

  • lives in forests
  • is most active at night
  • nests in abandoned cavities in trees
  • has one brood per year
  • is hunted by the Great Horned Owl
  • hoots “Who Cooks for You?” twice
  • doesn’t migrate
  • has acute sight and hearing
  • rips prey apart and eats the head first

Lanei

WESTERN SCREECH OWL (MEGASCOPS KENNICOTTII MACFARLANEI)

Lanei arrived at MARS in March 2015 from a research and captive breeding program for Western Screech Owls in Northern BC when he was about 2 years old. These birds are threatened and are protected under the federal Species at Risk Act. Lanei is non-releasable due to his inability to hunt or protect himself from predators. MARS’ late Ambassador Screech Owl, Otus Jr., was also from this same program.

Lanei is now a very old Western Screech Owl, and is mostly retired from work. Sometimes, he does show an eagerness to meet the public or go out for walks with his handlers, but for the most part, enjoys his peace and quiet.

Western Screech owls in captivity have a life expectancy of about 15 years. He eats one mouse each day and weighs 175 – 250 g. Lanei is a very quiet bird with a very sweet personality, and usually keeps his ear tufts down, so he often presents himself as a very well-rounded owl!

Western Screech Owl Cool Facts

  • makes a series of hollow toots
  • can take prey bigger than its own body
  • diet includes bats, insects and earthworms
  • invisible when pressing against a tree
  • live mainly in forested habitats
  • nest in woodpecker tree cavities
  • male and female often preen each other
  • male gets food for female and young in nest
  • vulnerable to predation from Barred Owls

Humpty

BALD EAGLE (HALIAEETUS LEUCOCEPHALUS)

Humpty was a pre-fledgling found at the base of a tree and thought to be dead. Five days later he was found to be moving and MARS was called in for rescue. As he had suffered a great fall, his finders named him Humpty.

Upon admission he was found to be suffering from severe dehydration and emaciation. His tail feathers were missing, but slowly grew back within his first year at MARS. It is also believed that he has some mental deficit due to his early injuries. He seldom vocalizes and his flight remains uncertain. His diet consists of poultry, fish and deer meat and he weighs about 3-3.5 kg.

Hyacinth

BALD EAGLE (HALIAEETUS LEUCOCEPHALUS)

Hyacinth (Hy) fell from her nest as a pre-fledgling when her nest fell apart. MARS was called and went by boat to the site at Hyacinth Cove on Quadra Island to rescue her.

She suffered multiple fractures in her right wing that were too close to the shoulder to be pinned so her wing was taped for 4 weeks. When another eagle accidentally bumped into her later, the wing was re-fractured. It eventually did heal but has a slight curve in the humerus so she is unable to “soar like an eagle”.

Hy is very vocal and likes to greet her handlers when they enter her enclosure. She is fairly gentle in spite of her size and gets along well with Humpty, her permanent roommate. She also seems to enjoy the company of other eagle patients when they are housed together. Her diet consists of poultry, fish and deer meat and she weighs about 3.5 – 4.0 kg.

Bald Eagle Cool Facts

  • not really bald, but their white-feathered heads gleam above their brown body
  • young birds attain adult plumage in about five years
  • scavenge many meals by harassing other birds or by eating carrion or garbage
  • eat mainly fish, but also hunt mammals, gulls, and waterfowl
  • known to play and have been seen passing sticks to each other in midair
  • the largest nest on record was 2.9 meters wide and 6.1 meters tall
  • the oldest recorded bird in the wild was at least 38 years old when it was hit and killed by a car

Nimpkish & Kokish

AMERICAN CROW (CORVUS BRACHYRHYNCHOS)

Siblings Nimpkish and Kokish arrived as juvenile birds in the summer of 2020. Finders reported that they were being aggressively mobbed by other crows. Kokish (female, on the right) had a greenstick fracture of the tibiotarsus, a leg bone. Her brother Nimpkish was brought to our hospital the next day as the attacks continued.

These distinctive birds are albinos, meaning they do not possess the pigments that would make their feathers, skin, beak, and eyes black. This genetic condition also results in very weak eyesight, extreme sensitivity to light, and poor depth perception, making it difficult for them to find food and protect themselves. Their feathers are easily damaged as they are missing the melanin that contributes both colour and strength. Because their feathers reflect light, albino birds have difficulty staying warm in winter. All these factors lead to a veterinarian determining that the pair would have the best quality of life by becoming Ambassadors at MARS.

Their names reflect their origin: these crows were born in the traditional territory of the ‘Namgis First Nation on northern Vancouver Island, which encompasses the Nimpkish and Kokish watersheds.

Nimpkish and Kokish have distinctive behaviours and a fascinating relationship. Please note: when temperatures fall below -10°C, these birds are moved into the hospital and aren’t available for viewing during tours.

American Crow Cool Facts

  • young crows will help their parents raise the next generation
  • form families of up to 15 individuals
  • scavenge many meals by harassing other birds or by eating carrion or garbage
  • prefer to nest in top branches of evergreens
  • are corvids, same family as the Stellar Jay, Canada Jay, and Common Raven found here
  • like all corvids, are extremely intelligent
  • have been observed making and using tools; can solve complex puzzles
  • are omnivores: will eat grains, nuts, berries, insects, eggs, nestlings, fish, carrion, and garbage

Kora

COMMON RAVEN (CORVUS CORAX)

Kora was a juvenile when she was found in distress in the spring of 2023. She was transferred to MARS in June of that year. Staff determined that she was completely blind in one eye and partially blind in the other, making her unreleasable. Kora’s sweet demeanor and adaptability around humans, even in her early days in care, made her a good candidate for the MARS ambassador program. As with all raven patients, our hospital team used the species-specific alpha code CORA (COmmon RAven) to identify her. The name stuck, and when she officially became part of the MARS education team, she was officially named Kora.

Very intelligent and playful like many of her species, Kora is quite a handful but has become used to her handlers. She greets them with a wide array of vocalizations and will play tricks on them, untying their shoes when the humans aren’t paying attention. As she is an omnivore, her diet consists of everything from berries to fish, but she has a soft spot for hard boiled eggs and mice. She weighs about 1kg.

Common Raven Cool Facts

  • ravens are among the smartest birds in the world. They’ve been seen using tools, solving puzzles, and even playing games with each other just for fun
  • these birds can mimic sounds from their environment
  • common Ravens mate for life and are known to form strong social bonds
  • ravens love to play in the air. They can flip, dive, and even fly briefly upside down
  • they’re found across the Northern Hemisphere, from deserts to forests to Arctic tundra
  • ravens have been important symbols in many cultures, often seen as messengers, tricksters, or guardians. For example, they’re sacred in many Indigenous cultures and are famously tied to Norse mythology

Athena

GREAT HORNED OWL (BUBO VIRGINIANUS)

Athena was admitted to MARS in August 2024 following a vehicle collision that left her brain damaged and visually impaired. The extent of her head injury left our staff wondering if she was completely blind.

After some time in care, her vision has improved, but her left eye remains less reactive than the right. We have grown to love her iconic uneven pupils (also known as Anisocoria). A vet confirmed that, while she has vision in both eyes, the left eye is only partially sighted and her depth perception is heavily compromised.

As hopeful as our hospital team was that she would continue to improve and become releasable, it was not to be. Since admission, Athena never regained the characteristics of a wild owl likely due to the severity of the head trauma. She could not pass her live prey test despite recovery time and treatment.

Our team recognized in her the qualities of an educational ambassador and applied for government permits to make her role official. Athena has adjusted successfully to training and has become a fast favourite of guests and handlers alike.

Athena’s healthy appetite and ability to swallow foods whole never cease to amaze us. Her preferred meal is mice, although she would never turn her beak up at the occasional quail.

Great Horned Owl Cool Facts

  • these bulky birds are the second in weight to Snowy Owls, the heaviest owls on the continent
  • the “horns” on these owls’ heads aren’t horns or ears. They are tufts of feathers
  • they can capture large birds like hawks and other owls, and mammals such as rabbits and squirrels, as well as smaller creatures like bats, mice, snakes, and frogs
  • these raptors are found in a variety of habitats across North America, including deserts, wetlands, forests, grasslands, backyards, and cities
  • a pair will adopt the nest of other large birds like ravens and Red-tailed Hawks or nest in snags, large cavities, or human-made platforms
  • they have one brood a year, laying one to four eggs

Gulliver

RED-TAILED HAWK (BUTEO JAMAICENSIS)

Gulliver was a very young hawk when he arrived at MARS in 2025. He was transferred to us from Pacific Northwest Raptors to join our team of educators. Although he’s not an ex-patient of MARS like most of our other ambassadors, Gulliver is a great fit with our team of feathered educators. This stunning hawk not only adds diversity to our native ambassadors, but to also helps “take the load off” our other hard-working gloved-trained birds who whohelp spread education and inspiration.

Gulliver may look fierce, but he is incredibly sweet natured. He flies confidently around his new digs, always coming to the perch by the door to greet his handlers with a tail wag.

Why Gulliver? His name comes from the word “glutton” from Middle English. [For the nerds: origin Old French gluton, from Latin glutto(n-) related to gluttire ‘to swallow’, gluttus ‘greedy’, and gula ‘throat’ Phew!] If you ever come to visit him and see Gulliver eating, you’ll see he indeed likes his food! As a Red-tailed Hawk, Gulliver is a carnivore, so he eats mostly mice and quail, but we believe he would attempt anything we dangle in front of his beak.

This young raptor weighs about 800g. We are very excited to watch this new bird learn and grow with MARS.

In Memoriam

Flying free but never forgotten

Sawyer (2014-2021)

NORTHERN SAW-WHET OWL (AEGOLIUS ACADICUS)

Sawyer had been with MARS since August of 2014 when he sustained damage to his right wing, possibly due to a cat attack. The damage to his wing was severe and the entire wing was surgically amputated. Because of his injuries he could not be released, so was adopted into the MARS ambassador family.

In 2016 Sawyer’s right eye became inflamed and even though it was treated with daily eye drops, it did not heal. In September 2017 he underwent surgical removal of his eye.

As a MARS ambassador, our little one-winged, one-eyed owl was among our fiercest birds. His small size and oversized personality made a lasting impression on all our visitors, young and old.

Sawyer’s job was to educate the public about owls and other wildlife, and help visitors feel a connection to the natural world. In the six years he spent as part of this program, he made an impact on the lives of hundreds of people of different ages from all over the Valley and beyond. He passed away at MARS in late January 2021 due to old age, surrounded by those who loved him.

Otus Jr (2014-2021)

WESTERN SCREECH OWL (MEGASCOPS KENNICOTTII KENNICOTTI)

Otus Jr. was the offspring of one of our previous ambassador owls who had participated in a captive breeding program for the threatened Western Screech Owl. Once the program was no more, Otus and his roommate, Lanei, were sent to MARS to live out their lives because they would not be able to fend for themselves in the wild.

Otus joined the MARS family in 2014 and became a mainstay of our education program. He visited local classrooms, attended community events, and did many on-site presentations during his lifetime. He was always content to get out and sit on the glove of a handler, many of whom shared an incredible bond with him. Handlers would often find Otus with his ear tufts up, ready to have a chat when they entered his enclosure to catch him up for his working day. He had a huge impact on all of us at MARS and had many fans out in the community.

In the end stages of his life, Otus became very ill with severe internal infections. After visits to several different vets and a number of tests that showed an increasingly poor prognosis, we decided that humane euthanasia was the kindest option for little Otus. He passed away at MARS in early February 2021, surrounded by those who loved and cared for him.

Shakespeare (2005-2023)

BARRED OWL (STRIX VARIA)

Shakespeare was hit by a car in 2003. He lost his left eye, fractured his beak and sternum, and had hearing loss on one side. He spent a year and a half in rehabilitation at the Orphaned Wildlife Rehabilitation Society in Delta, British Columbia (O.W.L.). His injuries would prevent him from fending for himself in the wild so it was not possible to release him.

Originally, Shakespeare was adopted by MARS as a foster parent to younger Barred Owls but he wasn’t very good in this role. He started training to become an Ambassador bird in 2006 and made his first public appearance six months later. He could often be heard calling to local wild owls late in the day, even making “howler monkey” calls on occasion. His diet was mostly quail and mice.

Over the 17 years he spent at MARS, Shakey touched the lives of thousands of people, helped with many events and school presentations, and bonded strongly with several of his handlers. He made a huge impact on the community during his time with us, and we are so grateful he was able to live out his life happily with us here at MARS.

Shakespeare developed liver disease and lung issues towards the end of his life and was humanely euthanized in March of 2023. He passed in the arms of his favourite handler.

We will miss you, Shakespeare. Fly high and free once again!

Brinley (2004-2023)

GREAT HORNED OWL (BUBO VIRGINIANUS)

Brinley was in full adult plumage when she was hit by a car in the Lower Mainland in 2005. Her right wing was so badly damaged that part of it had to be amputated. She was initially at the Vancouver Aquarium, then The Raptors in Duncan. As a non-flighted bird she needed a more suitable home for the long-term and thankfully, Maj and the team at MARS welcomed her in as an ambassador.

Jo, one of our caregivers, said: “Brinley was a force to be reckoned with. She chose whom to connect with and was the dominant partner in the relationship. Brinley lost the ability to be the apex predator of the night sky but never lost the attitude.” All of those that had the honour of having a progressing relationship with Brinley saw it as an immense privilege.

Great horned owls are known to have incredibly strong feet but those that worked with her on the glove claim she was the gentlest bird. She touched many lives while attending events and presentations, and became a regular to visit on our ambassador tour after her retirement. Her presence was truly amazing.

Brinley became ill in 2023, and due to her old age, she was humanely euthanized surrounded by those who loved her. We at MARS have had the absolute pleasure of working with Brinley, such an incredible species and personality. May she enjoy her final freedom in the skies.

Horus (2004-2023)

RED TAILED HAWK (BUTEO JAMAICENSIS)

Horus, known for her regal bearing and piercing gaze, was appropriately named after the falcon-headed Egyptian god of kingship, healing, protection, the sun, and the sky.

With her sharp eyes, commanding presence, and unforgettable personality, Horus captured the hearts of everyone she met. She was known for choosing her favorite handlers (and being stubborn about it!), delighting countless visitors, and educating thousands about the importance of protecting wildlife.

Horus came to MARS in 2008, having been discovered emaciated and in distress at the Quinsam River Hatchery in Campbell River. Her physical condition and her previous apparent imprinting on humans made her unreleasable.

Living a long and full life, far longer than she would have in the wild, Horus became one of the most recognized faces of MARS. Whether you met her at an event, during a visit, or saw her at one of the many organizations or schools she visited, you likely felt the same awe and respect she inspired in all of us. She passed away in 2025 surrounded by those who loved and would do anything for her.

She was a teacher, an inspiration, and a symbol of resilience. Fly free, Horus. You will never be forgotten.
